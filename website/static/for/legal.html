OpenExtract for legal professionals

Extract text messages, call history, and other iPhone data from a client's backup — formatted for exhibits, briefs, and production. Free and open source.

Most of the evidence that matters in family law, civil litigation, and increasingly in employment and harassment cases lives on someone's phone. When a client hands you their iPhone or a forensic image of their backup, you need the records out — timestamps intact, attachments included, readable in a binder and producible to opposing counsel.

That's the job OpenExtract was built for. It's free, MIT-licensed, and runs entirely on your own workstation. No cloud uploads, no vendor seeing your client's communications.

What you can extract

  • SMS and iMessage threads, with timestamps, sender/recipient info, and attachments preserved
  • Call history — incoming, outgoing, missed, durations
  • Contacts and calendar entries for establishing context
  • Photos and videos, with EXIF metadata (GPS coordinates, original timestamps) intact
  • Voicemails with transcription where iOS stored one
  • Notes, Safari history, saved passwords (encrypted backups only)
  • Third-party app data from WhatsApp, Signal, Telegram, and others
  • Threads the user deleted from their device after a backup was made — the backup still contains them, and OpenExtract reads them out

What OpenExtract does not do: forensic recovery of data that isn't in the backup. There is no SQLite free-page carving, no tombstoned-row reconstruction, no live-device acquisition. If a thread was deleted before the backup captured it, a specialist examiner with a vendor tool is the right next step — OpenExtract stays in its lane as a backup reader.

What "court-ready" means here

OpenExtract exports in three formats that are useful at different stages of a matter:

PDF — what you hand a judge. Full message threads rendered chronologically, with timestamps, attachments in-line, and a header identifying the export. Good for exhibits. Multi-page, auto-paginated, easy to Bates-stamp downstream.

CSV — what you hand an analyst or an expert. Rows, columns, every field accessible. Good for timeline analysis, communication-frequency graphs, and cross-referencing with other records.

HTML — what you hand yourself while preparing. Browseable archive, searchable locally, links between messages and attachments preserved. Good for discovery review.

All exports preserve original timestamps and metadata. Timezone information is retained. Attachment files are exported separately and linked, so hash verification against originals is possible.

Typical workflow

  1. Obtain the backup. Your client backs up their iPhone to their Mac (Finder) or Windows PC (iTunes / Apple Devices app), or you receive a forensic image from a retained expert. Encrypted backups are supported — just provide the password.
  2. Open the backup in OpenExtract. Point the application at the backup folder. On macOS this is typically ~/Library/Application Support/MobileSync/Backup/; on Windows, %APPDATA%\Apple Computer\MobileSync\Backup\.
  3. Select the data you need. Specific contacts, date ranges, message categories. You don't have to export everything.
  4. Export. Choose PDF for exhibits, CSV for analysis, or both.
  5. Produce or use in brief. The output is yours. OpenExtract retains nothing.

Common use cases we hear about

Family law — divorce, custody, support. Text message threads between spouses or between a parent and child frequently become central exhibits. Extracting the complete thread (not screenshots) establishes authenticity, preserves timestamps, and captures context that isolated screenshots strip out.

Civil litigation — breach of contract, fraud, tortious interference. Communication patterns between parties often establish what was known and when. A full export is more defensible than screenshots produced selectively.

Employment and harassment. Preservation of text-based evidence before a phone is returned, wiped, or reissued. An OpenExtract-produced PDF is the kind of artifact that can be placed into a preservation letter or litigation hold.

Estate matters and incapacity. Recovering communications and photos from a decedent's or incapacitated person's device when authorized by the estate or a guardian.

Honest limitations

OpenExtract is a free tool with real trade-offs you should be aware of:

  • It is not a chain-of-custody system. It extracts; it doesn't produce a formal chain-of-custody report, hash manifest, or examiner affidavit. If your matter requires that, retain a forensic examiner who uses dedicated forensics tooling — or supplement OpenExtract with a hash-manifest workflow.
  • It is not an expert witness. If the tool's behavior will be scrutinized on the stand, retain an expert who can speak to the extraction methodology. The fact that OpenExtract is open-source is an advantage here — the expert can actually read the code — but the expert still needs to be retained.
  • It works on backups, not live devices. If the device is passcode-locked and has never been backed up, OpenExtract has nothing to read. You'll need a forensic extraction or device-side workflow for that.
  • It doesn't bypass iCloud or account security. Data is what's in the backup; it isn't what Apple holds server-side. For iCloud-only data, subpoena or court order to Apple is the appropriate path.
  • No commercial support contract. Issues are filed on GitHub; response times are best-effort. If your matter has a deadline you'd bet a client relationship on, plan accordingly — either build in runway or keep a paid tool in your kit.

Privacy and client data

OpenExtract processes backups locally on the workstation it's installed on. It makes no network calls. The source code is public and auditable at github.com/charleswest775/openextract — you, your IT team, or an outside auditor can verify that.

For regulated environments (firms with information-governance or data-residency policies), this is often easier to clear than a SaaS tool that ships backups to a vendor's cloud.

Pricing

OpenExtract is free. MIT licensed. No per-user cost, no per-matter cost, no annual maintenance, no upgrade fees. You can install it on every workstation in the firm without procurement.

If you want to support the project, star it on GitHub or contribute improvements. There is no paid tier.
